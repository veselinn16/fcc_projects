Liverpool win the 6th

MADRID—For Liverpool to claw its way back to a Champions League final, one year after losing in one, the club had to play some of the best soccer in Europe for a year. It also had to sign the most expensive goalkeeper in the world, hold its nerve in places like Munich and Paris, and come back from the dead against Barcelona.

All told, the team managed by Jürgen Klopp would knock off the champions of four other European countries in its own extravagant style, just to reach Saturday’s game.

Then that style went missing. But despite delivering its ugliest performance in months, Liverpool somehow emerged from an all-English final to become champions of Europe for the sixth time. A pair of well-timed goals and a string of diving saves led, in the end, to a 2-0 victory over Tottenham Hotspur.

“I know how Tottenham feels in this moment, maybe better than anybody else in the world,” Klopp said. “They would have deserved this medal also, but we scored the goals in the right moment.”

Each one soothed Liverpool’s recent neuroses a little more. A year ago, there was the heartbreaking defeat to Real Madrid in another Champions League final that left it with a concussed goalkeeper and injured superstar. Less than a month ago, there was the gut punch of posting a staggering 97 points in the Premier League and still finishing second to Manchester City. Since Klopp arrived in 2015, Liverpool had lost three finals.

At the final whistle, the team sprinted to the end of the Estadio Metropolitano packed with the red-shirted Liverpool fans lucky enough to score a ticket. Many thousands more had traveled to Madrid without one and poured into the streets of the Spanish capital instead.

“This is only the start for this group,” Klopp said.

Though Premier League teams are known for their intensity, the game had a strangely subdued quality to it. Perhaps it was down to the three-week break between the end of the sides’ domestic campaigns and this final showpiece, an unheard of hiatus at this stage of the season. Perhaps it was down to the heat in Madrid, where temperatures topped 90 degrees on Saturday. But most likely, it was down to the manner of Liverpool’s opening goal: a controversial penalty kick.

Just 22 seconds into the game, Liverpool forward Sadio Mané burst into the Tottenham area and chipped a ball onto the top of midfielder Moussa Sissoko’s arm. The referee didn’t wait long to rule in Liverpool’s favor.

Interpreting the handball rule recently has become one of soccer’s thorniest on-field problems following the widespread introduction of instant replay to help referees. There are questions of intent and of arm position and how much either one matters. Much like the question of what constitutes a catch in the NFL these days, the number of people who fully grasp the nuances of the handball rule might be outnumbered by people who master chemical thermodynamics.

Liverpool’s Mohamed Salah might have been one of the few people in the stadium not asking himself any questions. He picked up the ball, drilled it through the middle of the goal and changed the tenor of the game.

In any other season under Klopp, Liverpool might have pressed aggressively for a second goal. Instead, in a season when it built new defensive solidity, the club did the opposite: It sat back, absorbed pressure, and trusted itself to keep Tottenham at bay. For the most part, it was comfortable work.

Only in the final 20 minutes did Liverpool have to rely on goalkeeper Alisson Becker to bail them out. (It wasn’t the only time this season: Alisson’s saves against Napoli and Barcelona were large factors in Liverpool reaching the final in the first place.) His most impressive contribution was a two-handed plunge to his left to parry a Christian Eriksen free-kick, just as Spurs seemed to gain a foothold in the game.

Tottenham would finish the game with twice as many shots on goal as Liverpool.

“He looked unbeatable, pretty much,” Klopp said of his goalkeeper.

Though Liverpool led for 88 minutes of regulation play, it took until the dying moments of the game for the fans to feel certain. After all, they hadn’t seen their team play such halting soccer in months. But Belgian striker Divock Origi, whose heroics against Barcelona in the semifinals were already the stuff of club legend, eased their nerves.

He lashed home Liverpool’s second goal with two minutes remaining in front of Liverpool’s fans, who won’t remember much about the quality of the game—only that they were going home with the trophy.
